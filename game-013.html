Q1
Quarter 1 @knowwhereyouare
Know where you are.

The thirteenth business lesson

Spatial awareness starts with knowing your position before making your decision.

A player who does not understand space becomes trapped by pressure. A player who reads space can create angles, protect options, and make the next move cleaner.

Space is information. The prepared player knows how to read it.
Q2
Quarter 2 @controlthespace
Control the space.

What space tells you

Every space on the floor has a message.

Open space, crowded space, weak-side space, strong-side pressure, driving lanes, passing windows, and defensive gaps all tell the player what is possible.

Open space

A place to attack, cut, pass, relocate, or create advantage.

Crowded space

A warning that pressure, help, or poor spacing may limit the next move.

Weak-side space

A place where the next advantage may quietly form.

Pressure space

A signal to protect the ball, move the ball, or change the angle.

Halftime @dontdribbleblind
Do not move blind.

Do not move before you read

Speed without spatial awareness can run straight into pressure.

Basketball business rule: movement is powerful only when the player understands where the advantage is.
Q3
Quarter 3 @anglescreateadvantage
Angles create advantage.

Angles, lanes, and timing

Spatial awareness helps the player create better angles before the defense reacts.

A small step can change a passing lane. A better angle can protect the ball. A timely cut can open the floor. A smarter relocation can turn pressure into advantage.

Passing angles

Change your position to make the pass easier, safer, and faster.

Driving lanes

Attack the space that gives you the cleanest advantage.

Relocation

Move without the ball to create better options for yourself and others.

Timing

The same space can be open or closed depending on when you move.

Q4
Quarter 4 @spacehabits
Build space habits.

Spatial awareness checklist

A serious player studies space before, during, and after the play.

1
Scan before receiving.

Know where pressure and options are before the ball arrives.

2
Know your outlet.

Every player should know where the safe next option is.

3
Move to create space.

Do not only use space. Create it for the team.

4
Review spacing mistakes.

Film can show when you were too close, too late, or in the wrong lane.
